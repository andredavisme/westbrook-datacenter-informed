Data Centers & Westbrook

A community resource to understand what data centers are, what they aren’t, and how Westbrook can shape what comes next.

Start Here

The Issue

📋

What Happened on May 18, 2026

The Westbrook City Council adopted a 180-day moratorium on data center development, enacted pursuant to 30-A M.R.S. § 3001 and § 4356. No official, officer, board or agency of the City may accept, process, approve, deny, or act upon any application relating to the siting, permitting, construction, or operation of a new, enlarged, or expanded data center during this period.

Source: Data Center Moratorium Ordinance, City of Westbrook, Maine — adopted May 18, 2026

⚖

Why the Council Acted: 8 Findings

The ordinance lists eight specific findings of fact:

  1. Recent increase in large-scale data center development in Maine
  2. Data centers consume disproportionate water & electricity — equivalent to tens of thousands of homes
  3. Data centers strain local energy grids and raise residential utility costs
  4. Data centers create noise pollution, heat islands, and significant environmental impacts
  5. Development without appropriate standards risks overburdening City resources
  6. Current Land Use Ordinance is inadequate to prevent serious public harm
  7. The City requires at least 180 days to gather information and develop regulations
  8. The foregoing facts necessitate a temporary moratorium

Source: Data Center Moratorium Ordinance, WHEREAS clauses 1–8

📍

Rock Row & the 2019 Zoning

In April 2019, the City Council approved Contract Zone 12 — Rock Row, a 66-acre mixed-use redevelopment of the former Pike Rock Quarry on Main Street. A data center was added as a permitted use by right at that time — the only place in Westbrook so zoned.

The moratorium definition of “data center” — a structure or facility that primarily contains electronic systems and equipment, such as servers, data storage devices and networking equipment, used to process, store or transmit digital information — is broader and more precise than the 2019 LUO definition.

Source: Westbrook City Council Packet, April 22, 2019 — Order 2019-58; Moratorium Ordinance §4

🗓

What Happens Next

During the 180-day window, the Planning Board will develop an amendment to the Westbrook Land Use Ordinance establishing appropriate standards for the siting, permitting, construction, and operation of data centers. The moratorium may be extended by the Council or repealed if standards are adopted sooner.

Violations carry fines, penalties, injunctive relief, and attorneys’ fees under 30-A M.R.S. § 4452.

Source: Moratorium Ordinance §§2, 5

Not All Data Centers Are the Same

A point made at the May 18 meeting is important: “data center” is a broad term. A facility built for one purpose will not require the same resources — or create the same impacts — as one built for another.

Impact varies enormously by type. The 180-day moratorium period is an opportunity for Westbrook to define which types, if any, are appropriate — and under what conditions.

Impact Factors: What to Weigh

These are the key dimensions any community should evaluate. Scale matters — context matters more.

⚡ Energy

Large data centers can draw as much electricity as tens of thousands of homes, stressing regional grids and raising residential utility costs. The energy source (renewable vs. fossil) dramatically changes the picture.

💧 Water

Evaporative cooling systems can consume millions of gallons per day. Air-cooled and closed-loop systems use significantly less. Maine’s water resources are not infinite.

💼 Jobs & Economy

Data centers create few permanent local jobs relative to their footprint. Construction jobs are temporary. Tax revenue can be significant — if the terms are right for the community.

🚗 Traffic & Infrastructure

Ongoing operational traffic is low. Construction phases generate significant heavy truck traffic. Rock Row’s proximity to I-95 exits 47 & 48 is a logistical asset but requires coordinated traffic management.

🔊 Noise & Light

Cooling systems (HVAC, chillers) generate continuous low-frequency noise. The 2019 Rock Row Contract Zone already required sound monitoring for the concert venue — the same standard could apply here.

🌿 Land & Environment

Rock Row sits on a former quarry — an already-disturbed industrial site. This is relevant context. Greenfield development carries a heavier environmental burden. Air quality depends on backup generator fuel type and frequency.

Rock Row: What Was Promised

The 2019 Rock Row Master Plan (Waterstone Properties) envisioned a 2.5 million sq ft mixed-use destination including retail, restaurants, a hotel, residential units, an amphitheater, office space — and a data center as a permitted use. The vision: transform a 100-year-old quarry into a gateway for Westbrook and the region, drawing an estimated 5 million annual visitors.

The community’s concern is not with data centers in the abstract — it’s with ensuring any proposal fits that vision, rather than replacing it with something that serves a corporation at the community’s expense.

Source: Rock Row Contract Zone 12, Exhibit A Master Plan, March 7, 2019

What Could Be

The 180-day window is not just a pause — it’s a blank page. Two futures are possible. The difference is whether Westbrook shows up to write the terms.

⚠

If We Don’t Get This Right

The real risks — not fears, but documented outcomes from communities that moved too fast or negotiated too little.

  • Utility bills go up for everyoneGrid strain from large data centers has driven residential electricity rate increases in communities across Virginia, Georgia, and the Midwest. Westbrook is already a working-class city with tight household budgets.
  • Water drawn from a shared resourceMaine’s aquifers and rivers are not infinite. Large-scale evaporative cooling in a drought year is a community-wide problem, not just a corporate one.
  • Noise that never stopsHVAC and chiller systems run 24/7/365. Low-frequency hum travels farther than most people expect. Residents near data centers in other states have fought — and lost — battles over chronic noise after the permits were signed.
  • Jobs that aren’t localWithout contract terms, the 30–50 permanent operations staff may be recruited nationally, leaving Westbrook residents no closer to employment than before the facility opened.
  • Tax revenue that doesn’t lastDepreciation schedules, TIF agreements, and data center equipment devaluation can dramatically reduce assessed value within 5–10 years — leaving the City holding infrastructure costs without the revenue to match.
  • Heat islands near residential areasWaste heat from cooling systems raises ambient temperatures in surrounding blocks. Near housing and schools, that’s a public health issue, not just a nuisance.
  • The Rock Row vision quietly replacedA data center footprint that displaces the mixed-use master plan — the hotel, the housing, the amphitheater — gives Westbrook a facility instead of a destination. That trade is permanent.
  • No exit strategyData centers become obsolete. Without decommissioning requirements in the contract, Westbrook could be left with a vacated industrial shell on its most prominent development parcel.
✨

If We Do Get This Right

None of this has been done at scale — because no community has demanded it yet. Westbrook could be the first to prove that a data center can mean something completely different.

  • High school internship pipelineA negotiated community benefit agreement could require the operator to host Westbrook High School students in structured, paid internships — in networking, cybersecurity, facilities management, and data operations. Not a tour. A career pathway.
  • Global business insights, local classroomThe engineers, executives, and operators who run a data center come from a world most Westbrook students never get to see up close. Guest lecture programs, job shadowing, and mentorship arrangements cost the operator almost nothing — and could change a kid’s trajectory.
  • University research partnershipsUSM, UMaine, and Maine Tech Institute could access real infrastructure for research in energy efficiency, cooling technology, and AI systems — with the data center as a living lab. Maine doesn’t have to just host the future; it can study it.
  • Waste heat recovery for community useData centers produce enormous amounts of waste heat. In Helsinki and Stockholm, that heat already warms entire residential districts. Westbrook could negotiate district heating rights — reducing home heating costs for nearby residents from day one.
  • Technology sharing as infrastructure evolvesHardware refresh cycles happen every 3–5 years. Decommissioned servers, networking gear, and workstations — still functional — could flow to Westbrook schools, the public library, and community organizations instead of landfills or resellers.
  • Renewable energy anchor for the gridA data center committed to 100% renewable power becomes an anchor customer for Maine’s clean energy industry — potentially attracting solar and wind investment that benefits the whole region’s grid.
  • Workforce training fundA required annual contribution to a City-administered workforce development fund — negotiated as a condition of approval — could fund trades certification, coding bootcamps, and IT certifications for Westbrook adults for years.
  • A model for every town watchingCommunities across Maine — and the country — are facing this same decision right now. If Westbrook writes a community benefit agreement that actually works, it becomes the template. That’s a kind of leadership that doesn’t require being the biggest city in the room.

💡 The window is open. These aren’t wishes — they’re negotiating positions. Every item on the opportunity list has been achieved somewhere, by someone, who decided to ask. Westbrook has 180 days to decide what it wants to ask for.
